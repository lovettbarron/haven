# Soil Layer Guide

How to fill each raised bed with a three-layer system: drainage at the bottom, garden waste in the middle, and hojbedsmuld on top. Each layer has a specific purpose -- drainage prevents waterlogging, garden waste provides slow-release nutrients and reduces the volume of expensive purchased soil, and hojbedsmuld is the growing medium where roots live.

Both beds are bottomless on grass. No LECA or coconut coir is needed.

---

## Bed A -- "Family Bed" (230x120x40cm, on grass)

Total volume: 1,104 liters. This bed has two zones with different bottom layers.

### South Zone (L=0-120cm, reservoir)

The south zone houses the warm crops (tomato, cucumber, pepper, basil) and uses a self-watering reservoir instead of drainage gravel. Zone volume: 120x120x40 = 576 liters.

**Bottom Layer: Reservoir (~10cm)**
The drainage gravel layer is replaced by the self-watering reservoir system. See [reservoir-build.md](reservoir-build.md) for the bottom ~10cm construction (liner, gravel, pipes).

**Middle Layer: Garden Waste (10-15cm)**
Loosely packed branches, leaves, grass clippings, and straw above the reservoir's geotextile layer.

**Top Layer: Hojbedsmuld (15-20cm)**
Same hojbedsmuld as all other zones. Level and lightly tamp.

### Standard Zones (L=120-230cm, middle + north)

The middle zone houses herbs, salad, broccoli, and leeks. The north zone houses raspberries. Both use standard 3-layer drainage. Combined zone volume: 120x110x40 = 528 liters.

**Bottom Layer: Drainage Gravel (8-10cm)**
Spread 8-10cm of coarse gravel (16-32mm singels) evenly across the bed floor. This layer prevents waterlogging during Danish autumn rains by allowing excess water to drain freely through the bed and into the grass below.

**Middle Layer: Garden Waste (10-15cm)**
Fill 10-15cm with a mix of organic garden waste:

- **Branches** -- break or cut to 10-20cm lengths
- **Fallen leaves** -- partially decomposed are best
- **Grass clippings** -- mix with coarser material to avoid matting
- **Straw** -- good for creating air pockets

Pack loosely rather than compressing tightly. This layer works on the hugelkultur principle: the organic material decomposes slowly over the season, releasing nutrients into the growing layer above. It also saves 30-40% on purchased soil.

**Top Layer: Hojbedsmuld (15-20cm)**
Fill the remaining depth with hojbedsmuld (purchased raised bed soil mix). Level the surface and lightly tamp with your hands -- do not compress heavily. This is the growing medium where seeds are sown and roots develop.

> **Note:** There is no physical divider between the south zone and the standard zones. The reservoir liner in the south zone creates a natural boundary at the bottom layer. The garden waste and hojbedsmuld layers are continuous across the full 230cm length.

### Material Summary

- **Hojbedsmuld needed:** Approximately 549-732 liters (0.55-0.73 m3) for all top layers across both beds. Order 0.7-0.8 m3 to allow for settling and top-ups.
- **Drainage gravel needed:** Approximately 178-222 liters (0.18-0.22 m3) for Bed A standard zones and Bed B. The Bed A south zone uses the reservoir system instead of gravel.
- **Garden waste needed:** Approximately 366-549 liters for the middle layers of both beds. Sourced free from your own garden -- branches, leaves, grass clippings.

## Tips

- **Water thoroughly after filling.** Soak each bed with a hose or watering can until water drains from the bottom. This settles the layers and removes air pockets.
- **Expect settling.** The soil level will drop 5-10cm over the first few weeks as the garden waste compresses and the hojbedsmuld settles. Top up with additional hojbedsmuld after 2-3 weeks.
- **Fill beds at least 1 week before planting** to allow initial settling and for the soil to reach a consistent moisture level.
- **Child involvement:** Spreading the top hojbedsmuld layer is an excellent task for a 7-year-old -- it is manageable, visible progress, and does not require precision. Let him level it with his hands or a small rake.
