# Year 2 Soil Refresh Guide

**Project:** Haven -- A Learning Garden
**Location:** Vejle, Denmark (USDA Zone 7b)
**Applies to:** Bed A (230x120x40cm) and Bed B (150x60x40cm)

A two-step protocol for refreshing raised bed soil between growing seasons: fall amendment after cleanup, and spring top-dressing before planting. This guide builds on the original soil recipe in `docs/soil-layers.md` and addresses the normal soil volume loss that occurs as the garden waste middle layer decomposes.

**Cross-references:**
- `docs/soil-layers.md` -- original 3-layer soil recipe (drainage, garden waste, hojbedsmuld)
- `docs/end-of-season-guide.md` -- end-of-season cleanup countdown (Day 3 references this guide)
- `docs/perennial-care.md` -- perennial plant overwintering instructions

## Why Soil Needs Refreshing

Raised bed soil depletes in two ways:

1. **Volume loss:** The garden waste middle layer (branches, leaves, grass clippings) decomposes over the growing season. This is the hugelkultur layer described in `docs/soil-layers.md`. As it breaks down, it releases nutrients but also shrinks -- expect the soil level to drop **5-10cm** by the end of Year 1. This is completely normal and expected.

2. **Nutrient depletion:** Heavy-feeding crops (tomatoes, cucumbers, peppers) extract significant nutrients from the top hojbedsmuld layer. Without amendment, Year 2 yields will decline.

The two-step protocol addresses both issues: fall compost feeds soil biology over winter, spring top-dressing restores volume and nutrients.

---

## Step 1: Fall Amendment (W44-W46, After Cleanup)

Do this after plant removal on Day 2 of the end-of-season countdown (see `docs/end-of-season-guide.md`).

### Procedure

1. **Rake the bed surface smooth** after removing spent annual crops. Remove any large root clumps, stones, or debris.

2. **Spread 2-5cm of compost** evenly over the entire bed surface. This can be:
   - Bagged havekompost from a garden center
   - Homemade garden compost if you have a compost pile
   - A mix of both

3. **Cover with 5-10cm of straw mulch.** The straw insulates the soil and compost through winter, maintaining soil organism activity deeper into the cold months. It also suppresses weed growth and prevents erosion from winter rain.

4. **Do not dig or turn the soil.** Let the compost sit on the surface -- earthworms and soil organisms will pull it down naturally over winter. This is called "no-dig" amendment.

### Bed-Specific Notes (Fall)

**Bed A north zone (reservoir):**
- Amend the same way as standard zones. The compost and mulch sit on the top hojbedsmuld layer, above the geotextile/gravel reservoir system. No special handling needed.
- Reservoir should be drained before winter (see `docs/end-of-season-guide.md` Day 3).

**Bed A south zone (raspberries):**
- Apply compost around but not directly on raspberry canes. Leave 5cm clearance around each cane base.
- Raspberry zone gets additional compost in February after cane pruning (see `docs/perennial-care.md`).

**Bed B:**
- Same procedure. Compost around but not directly on perennial plant crowns (strawberries, thyme, lamb's ear, lemon balm pot).

## Step 2: Spring Amendment (W14-W15, Before Planting)

Do this 1-2 weeks before spring planting day (W16).

### Procedure

1. **Remove or rake aside winter mulch.** The straw may be partially decomposed by spring -- that is fine. Rake it off the bed surface and either compost it or set it aside to use as summer mulch around plants later.

2. **Check the soil level.** Measure from the soil surface to the top rim of the bed. In Year 1 beds, expect the soil to be **5-10cm below the rim** due to garden waste layer decomposition. This is normal -- not a sign of a problem.

3. **Top-dress with fresh hojbedsmuld.** Add enough raised bed soil mix (hojbedsmuld) to bring the soil level back to 2-3cm below the bed rim. This is typically:
   - **Bed A:** 5-10cm across the full bed, roughly 140-280 liters
   - **Bed B:** Up to 10cm, roughly 45-90 liters (smaller volume depletes faster)

4. **Optional: soil test before planting.** A simple pH test confirms the soil is in the right range for vegetables (6.0-7.0). See the soil testing section below for details.

5. **Boost heavy feeder zones.** For zones where tomatoes, cucumbers, peppers, or other heavy feeders will be planted:
   - Mix in an additional 2-3cm of compost to the top 10cm of soil, OR
   - Apply slow-release granular fertilizer (havegodning/langtidsgodning) per package instructions

6. **Level and lightly tamp.** Do not compress heavily -- just firm the surface gently with hands or the back of a rake. Water lightly to settle the new soil.

### Bed-Specific Notes (Spring)

**Bed A north zone (reservoir):**
- Before adding new soil, check the reservoir system. Pour water into the fill tube and confirm it flows to the overflow. Listen for splashing -- if water flows freely, the pipes survived winter. If blocked, investigate before planting.
- The reservoir stays wetter than other zones, which can cause the soil above to compact more. Gently loosen the top 5cm with a hand fork before top-dressing.
- New hojbedsmuld sits on the existing soil/geotextile system. No need to disturb the reservoir layer.

**Bed A middle zone:**
- Standard top-dress. This zone benefits from afternoon shade and stays moister than the north zone.

**Bed A south zone (raspberries):**
- Apply top-dress around canes (which will be stubs after February pruning). This is also where the post-pruning compost from February is already sitting.
- Raspberry canes will push through the new soil quickly.

**Bed B:**
- Bed B has 360 liters total volume -- the smallest bed. It depletes faster and may need the full 10cm of top-dressing.
- Monitor Bed B soil quality more closely during the season. If plants look pale or stunted by midsummer, a liquid feed (tomato fertilizer) can supplement.
- Top-dress around established perennials (strawberry crowns, thyme, lamb's ear). Do not bury the crowns -- they should remain at or slightly above the new soil level.

## Optional: Soil Testing

Soil testing is optional but informative, especially after Year 1 when the garden waste layer has been decomposing and altering soil chemistry.

### Simple pH Test (Recommended)

**What you need:** A pH test kit from any garden center (30-50 DKK). These use color-changing solution or indicator strips.

**How to test:**
1. Take a small soil sample from the top 10cm of each bed (or each zone if you want more detail).
2. Mix the soil with the test solution per kit instructions.
3. Compare the color to the chart provided.

**What to look for:**
- **Ideal range for vegetables:** pH 6.0-7.0
- **Below 6.0 (too acidic):** Add garden lime (havekalk) per package instructions. Straw mulch and compost decomposition can acidify soil over time.
- **Above 7.0 (too alkaline):** Add sulphur or more compost. Unlikely in Danish conditions.
- **6.0-7.0:** No adjustment needed. Carry on.

### Full Nutrient Analysis (Optional)

For a comprehensive picture (pH, nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, organic matter), use a postal soil testing service:

**Eurofins Miljoe:** Denmark's primary environmental testing laboratory. Order a soil test kit online, collect samples per their instructions, post them in, and receive results by email. Cost is approximately 300-500 DKK depending on the analysis package.

**When this is worthwhile:**
- If plants showed signs of nutrient deficiency in Year 1 (yellowing leaves, poor fruit set, stunted growth)
- If you want a baseline before making specific fertilizer decisions
- Every 2-3 years as a general check-up

**When it is not necessary:**
- If Year 1 went well and plants were healthy and productive
- If you are already adding compost and fresh hojbedsmuld -- these provide a balanced nutrient profile for most vegetable gardens

### Amendment Recommendations Based on Test Results

| Deficiency | Symptom | Amendment | Danish Product |
|-----------|---------|-----------|----------------|
| Nitrogen (N) | Pale/yellow leaves, slow growth | Compost, blood meal, or slow-release NPK | Havekompost, blodmel, or havegodning |
| Phosphorus (P) | Purple-tinted leaves, poor fruiting | Bone meal or rock phosphate | Benmel |
| Potassium (K) | Brown leaf edges, weak stems | Wood ash (small amounts) or potash | Traaske (sparsely), kaliumgodning |
| Low pH | General poor growth | Garden lime | Havekalk |

**Default approach if you skip testing:** Add 2-5cm compost (fall) + 5-10cm fresh hojbedsmuld (spring) + slow-release fertilizer in heavy feeder zones. This covers most nutrient needs for a home garden without precision testing.

## Key Reminders

- **5-10cm soil drop is normal.** Do not panic when beds look under-filled in spring. This is the garden waste layer doing its job -- decomposing and feeding the soil.
- **Compost before mulch in fall.** The compost needs contact with the soil surface to feed organisms. Straw goes on top as insulation.
- **Do not dig the reservoir zone.** Top-dress only. The geotextile and gravel system underneath must remain undisturbed.
- **Bed B depletes faster.** Monitor it more closely. Its smaller volume means proportionally greater nutrient loss per growing season.
- **Hojbedsmuld is the primary top-dress.** It is the same raised bed soil mix used in Year 1. Compost supplements it but does not replace it for volume restoration.
